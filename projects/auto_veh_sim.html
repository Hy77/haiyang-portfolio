ROS-Based Autonomous Ackerman Track Racing

A ROS-based autonomous driving simulation pipeline using laser perception, cone-based road-centre validation, and Ackerman steering control for track racing in Gazebo and RViz.

Problem

Build a ROS node that uses laser data to detect cones, validate road-centre goals, and autonomously complete a simulated racing lap.

My Role

Designed the ROS perception-control pipeline, implemented laser-based cone validation, steering logic, braking behaviour, and mission service lifecycle.

Outcome

Achieved stable multi-goal waypoint tracking with real-time steering updates, braking control, and service-based mission management.

ROS System Design

The system was developed as a ROS node subscribing to vehicle odometry, laser scan data, and externally supplied goals. Control outputs were published as steering, throttle, and brake commands for the Ackerman platform.

A service interface was implemented to start and terminate the mission safely, allowing the vehicle to remain idle until explicitly activated.

Cone Perception and Goal Validation

Front-facing laser scan data was used to detect cone pairs defining the track boundaries. The perception module verified whether each candidate goal lay within the drivable region formed by the cone boundaries.

This ensured that only safe and valid road-centre waypoints were passed to the controller, improving mission reliability and preventing invalid trajectory execution.

Ackerman Control Execution

The controller continuously computed steering commands toward the next validated goal. Throttle and braking logic were adjusted based on proximity to the waypoint and steering demand.

Additional safeguards handled extreme steering angles and mission abort conditions, ensuring robust control execution during tight turns and sparse cone layouts.

Mission Service Testing

A ROS service endpoint was implemented to explicitly start and terminate the racing mission. This allowed controlled lifecycle management, easier debugging, and safer simulation resets during repeated testing.

Service testing confirmed correct transitions between idle, running, and stopped states while preserving vehicle stability.

Key Findings

This project strengthened my experience in ROS node architecture, real-time perception-control integration, laser-based geometric reasoning, and simulation-oriented validation workflows.

The most valuable engineering lesson was designing a modular autonomy stack where sensing, goal validation, control, and mission lifecycle management could be tested independently yet operate robustly as a unified system.
