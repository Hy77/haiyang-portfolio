Geometric Feature Analysis of Nasal Airflow Images

A clinical image analysis workflow for extracting geometric features from nasal airflow images and linking them to objective airway resistance and subjective symptom scores.

Problem

Transform noisy clinical airflow images into reliable geometric representations for quantitative analysis.

My Role

Designed the image processing pipeline, skeleton extraction, feature engineering framework, and predictive modelling workflow.

Outcome

Built a robust methodology that extracted large-scale geometric features and revealed strong links with objective airway resistance measurements.

Methodology Overview

This project developed a structured pipeline for analysing nasal airflow images from clinical measurements. The workflow standardised input images, removed labels and grid artifacts, extracted clean skeleton curves, grouped the nasal geometry into left and right structures, and computed a large set of geometric, asymmetry, curvature, derivative, and frequency-based features.

Image Preprocessing

Input images were standardised to a consistent size, enhanced through brightness and contrast adjustment, and cleaned by removing fixed labels and measurement markers. Grid lines were then reduced to isolate the clinically relevant flow structure for later geometric analysis.

Skeletonization & Coordinate Structuring

The cleaned image was converted into a single-pixel skeleton using thinning-based morphology. Additional filtering removed artifacts and tail edges, while the central intersection point was estimated to separate the geometry into left and right curve groups for consistent downstream analysis.

Polynomial & Geometric Feature Extraction

Multi-degree polynomial fitting was used to model the nasal cavity geometry at different levels of detail. Beyond raw coefficients, the framework extracted curvature, tortuosity, asymmetry, energy, derivative, Fourier, width, and angular features, generating hundreds of quantitative descriptors per image.

Clinical Correlation Analysis

Extracted geometric features were evaluated against both subjective symptom scores and objective nasal airway resistance (NAR) measurements. While no strong correlation was observed with patient-reported symptom scores, the polynomial and asymmetry-based features showed strong relationships with objective NAR values. This provides an important technical validation that the extracted geometric representation is physiologically meaningful, even if its relationship with subjective symptoms remains unclear.

The absence of a strong subjective correlation may reflect limited sample size, variability in patient-reported scoring, or a genuinely weak relationship between perceived symptoms and measurable airflow geometry.

Key Findings

The pipeline generated approximately 500–800 geometric descriptors per image across polynomial, asymmetry, curvature, derivative, and frequency domains. The strongest validation signal came from objective NAR measurements, indicating that the extracted polynomial geometry reliably captures measurable airflow structure.

In contrast, no clear correlation was found with subjective symptom scores. This does not necessarily invalidate the methodology; rather, it suggests that patient-reported obstruction may depend on additional physiological, neurological, or perceptual factors beyond the geometric patterns captured in these airflow images.

Another possibility is that the current dataset size was insufficient to reveal weaker subjective relationships. Regardless, the strong objective validation supports the technical correctness of the polynomial-based feature extraction framework.

Feature Scale

Hundreds of features per image across morphology, asymmetry, curvature, derivatives, and frequency domains.

Strongest Signal

Objective NAR measurements showed very strong geometric relationships, with correlations reported up to 0.949.

Clinical Insight

Extracted geometric descriptors were compared against both subjective symptom scores and objective nasal airway resistance (NAR) measurements. The strongest correlations were consistently observed with objective NAR values.

Future Direction

Future work will focus on expanding the clinical dataset and integrating additional multimodal inputs, such as patient symptom history and complementary physiological measurements, to better investigate the weak relationship between image-derived geometry and subjective symptom perception.

On the technical side, the feature engineering framework can be extended with more advanced shape descriptors and learned representations, providing a stronger basis for predictive modelling and clinical decision support.
