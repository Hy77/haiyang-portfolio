Modular Pipe Inspection Robot Drive System

An industry-sponsored modular robot prototype designed for inspection-oriented pipe navigation, featuring Mecanum-based drive, flexible inter-module connection, and compact electromechanical integration for 150 mm pipe environments.

Problem

Develop a compact modular robot that can navigate inside 150 mm pipes, move in multiple directions, handle bends and 4-way intersections, and support future inspection and repair use cases.

My Role

Motor integration, embedded control setup, inter-module joint design, cable routing, prototype iteration, and validation testing.

Outcome

Built and validated a drive-capable robot module with a redesigned joint that achieved full bending motion, modular maintenance, and stable pipe navigation performance.

Project Context

This project was developed in response to a real utility infrastructure problem: enabling robotic platforms to move through water pipes for future inspection and maintenance tasks. The design had to fit within a 150 mm pipe while supporting forward and backward motion, rotation in place, bend navigation, and eventual traversal of 4-way intersections.

The system architecture used two compact robot sections connected through a flexible joint. Each section integrated wheels, motors, structure, and embedded electronics within a constrained chassis envelope.

Drive Module and Wheel Selection

A major early design decision was the drive strategy. The module needed multidirectional motion and controllability while maintaining top-bottom contact inside the pipe. After comparing candidate wheel types, Mecanum wheels were selected as the most balanced option for mobility, size, traction, and controllability under the project constraints.

This selection supported lateral motion, in-place turning, and smoother navigation through confined pipe sections compared with alternative wheel concepts. The pipe robot design notes also highlighted the tight spatial constraints and the need to preserve room for suspension and chassis components.

Inter-Module Joint Design

The connection joint between the two modules was a key subsystem. It needed to support bending, hold the robot securely during climbing, allow fast assembly and removal, and provide a protected path for cables between sections.

The first iteration used a pin-based swivel concept, but testing showed deformation under load and poor cable routing. I then redesigned the connection into a hook-and-shaft mechanism with an internal cable channel, improved geometry, and passive restoring support. This second iteration achieved a much stronger and more maintainable solution.

Motor Integration and Embedded Control

I integrated the motors with the Arduino Nano and PCA9685 control board, including wiring, power routing, servo channel allocation, and speed control logic. During early testing, all motors running simultaneously caused voltage drop and intermittent response.

To solve this, I implemented a staggered startup strategy so motors were activated in pairs with short delays. This improved voltage stability and gave consistent motor response while staying within the power budget of the system.

Validation in Pipe Test Rig

The prototype was tested in a transparent pipe rig to verify system-level behaviour. Validation targets included fit within a 150 mm pipe, forward and backward motion, in-pipe rotation, bend compatibility, modular installation/removal, and load-bearing stability during operation.

Pipe Fit

Validated against the 150 mm pipe constraint with compact chassis and drive layout.

Joint Performance

The revised joint achieved full bending capability and supported higher load without deformation.

System Reliability

Integrated wiring, cable routing, and motor startup logic improved repeatability and maintenance.

Key Findings

This project demonstrated the value of iterative electromechanical prototyping under realistic constraints. The strongest engineering lesson came from subsystem redesign: the initial joint concept was insufficient under load and cable-routing requirements, but targeted iteration led to a much more robust connection architecture. On the control side, small embedded changes such as staggered startup had a significant impact on real hardware reliability.

Overall, the project strengthened my experience in modular robot design, hardware-software integration, 3D printed mechanical iteration, and validation-oriented engineering development.

Future Direction

Future work would focus on expanding autonomy and robustness, including improved sensing, leak-detection payload integration, waterproofing considerations, and more advanced navigation through complex pipe networks. The current prototype provides a solid mechanical and control foundation for those later inspection-oriented upgrades.
