36th WARMAN Competition Robot Design and Integration

A competition-driven mechatronics robot designed for ball collection, silo placement, and stable mobile manipulation under strict size, weight, and time constraints.

Competition Track Context

Problem

Design and build a robot under competition constraints to collect balls and place them into silos within a limited time.

My Role

Team leader, chassis designer, lead programmer, and contributor to system integration, component selection, and testing.

Outcome

Developed an integrated robot platform combining mobile base, anti-rollover support, robotic arm extension, and embedded control logic.

System Overview

The WARMAN robot was designed to collect tennis and squash balls and place them into silos of different heights under strict competition limits on footprint, weight, and task time. The final system combined a Mecanum-wheel mobile base, a robotic arm, a collector mechanism, and anti-rollover support to improve stability during arm extension and placement.

Requirement-Driven Design Process

The design process used requirement analysis, morphological tables, and scoring matrices to evaluate alternatives for chassis layout, support mechanisms, manufacturability, weight, cost, and stability. This helped guide concept selection in a structured engineering way rather than relying only on intuition.

Base and Anti-Rollover Mechanism

A major design challenge was maintaining stability while the arm extended toward the silo. The chassis incorporated anti-rollover protection and later evolved into an extendable support mechanism to improve the robot’s stability without violating competition size limits.

Embedded Control and Mecanum Drive

The robot was controlled using an Arduino Mega2560 with TB6612-based motor driving logic. The control structure supported Mecanum wheel motion, arm actuation, collection control, and coordinated task execution through modular embedded code.

System Integration

The final robot integrated mechanical structure, wiring, control electronics, and motion logic into a complete competition-ready system. This project strengthened my experience in design-for-manufacture, hardware-software integration, and iterative testing under real constraints.

Key Takeaways

This project was a strong exercise in full-cycle mechatronics engineering: translating task requirements into mechanical concepts, validating design trade-offs, building the robot, and implementing the embedded control logic needed to coordinate the full system.
